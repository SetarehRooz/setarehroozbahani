About

To reach and face the unknown future, we need to challenge the existing ways of research, knowledge, and understanding. With the multiple layers of realities of this integrated environment, this uncertain future, requires empowering human (and non-human) institutions to face this fragility.

4:23 in the morning, as I slowly woke from a labyrinth of dreams— in a constant fleeing from the giant officer, I came across a small arch, just low enough to hide beneath. There in the dim yellow light, I encountered a little monk, swirling a glowing potion in an oval-shaped pot. His hands moved slowly, counterclockwise. He caught my gaze, pausing for a brief, as if sensing the trouble that was about to grow. The officer burst into the room, searching. The little tilted his head, feigning ignorance of my presence. The giant hesitated, then turned and left. The dream lingered, as I woke back to the reality of routine, … "of rapid waking, of dream and soon of death" (Virilio, P., Crary, J. and Beitchman, P. (2009) The Aesthetics of Disappearance. Los Angeles, CA: Semiotext.)

Each evening, as I walk home after long working hours, crossing the Sint-Michiels Bridge to the bus station, I find myself lost in thought—dreaming of an escape from routine, of pushing beyond my existing boundaries. I dream of research as an act of empowerment, a window through which raw ideas can evolve into profound explorations. A practice-based approach that not only allows for self-growth but fosters the exchange of collective knowledge in an interdisciplinary space.

During my master's studies, I explored various subjects within architecture, particularly its intersections with art, aesthetics, and broader socio-political, technological, and environmental contexts. I have always believed that a thin line separates fluid and ever—evolving disciplines. My academic journey deepened my interest in computational design thinking and digital tools such as 3D scanning, photogrammetry, and sensor-driven data integration in physical space, offering unique modes of expression and spatial exploration. However, after graduation, I often encounter the distinct gap between speculative, research-driven architectural inquiry and the firm framework of traditional architectural practice. Through internships and professional experiences, I realized how the industry’s focus on the norms of construction sometimes leaves little room for critical speculation, where architecture plays not only as a drafter of built, but imagined of a questioned. This realization has strengthened my determination to pursue research that engages with space beyond its ordinary form, deepening into the multidisciplinary dimensions.

I seek thorough guidance to develop my practice within critical fields and prepare for a possible research projects. I am eager to immerse myself in an interdisciplinary environment where the collectiveness of knowledge and ideas fosters new perspectives, where forensic spatial analysis, speculative drawing, and digital technologies create new ways of seeing and understanding. Since 2019, through my professional experience in architectural practice, I have gained valuable insight into architectural detailing and technical coordination, while continuously investigating how digital workflows can connect practice with emerging research fields, including artificial intelligence.

In parallel, I am exploring multiple directions within spatial practice and computation: from the integration of microcontrollers and coding, to how AI and computational methods can support reasoning and the generation of knowledge in design process. I am also drawn to computational image-making in the register of uncertainty, messy, incomplete drawings, not as a failure of precision, but as analytical tools for spatial inquiry, pushing beyond the boundaries of the built and unbuilt.

What connects all of this is a belief that practice and research are not separate, that making and questioning belong together. I am looking for an environment where that kind of thinking is taken seriously: where the incomplete, the speculative, and the unknown are not problems to solve, but places to begin. Where we remain the the creators of tools, not only their users.
